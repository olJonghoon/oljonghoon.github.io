On June 3, 2026, South Korea’s ninth nationwide local elections were disrupted by an unprecedented ballot shortage. 
Beginning around 1 p.m., polling stations across Seoul’s Songpa District started running out of ballots, leaving voters waiting in long lines. 
While some precincts received emergency deliveries of additional ballots, 
others failed to secure new supplies before the official close of voting. 
At those polling stations, election officials issued queue numbers to waiting voters and extended voting hours until 10 p.m. on an exceptional basis. 

According to the 
<a href="https://img.nec.go.kr/cmm/dozen/view.do?cbIdx=1090&bcIdx=326957&fileNo=1" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer">National Election Commission (NEC)</a>, 
91 polling stations nationwide required emergency deliveries of additional ballots. 
Of those, voting was temporarily suspended at 26 locations, 
while 14 still had voters waiting in line after the official close of polls. 
<a href="https://news.kbs.co.kr/news/pc/view/view.do?ncd=8577597" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer">All 14 of those polling stations</a> 
were concentrated in Seoul: 
12 in Songpa District, one in Gangnam District, and one in Gwangjin District. 

<a href="https://www.ytn.co.kr/_ln/0101_202606032208124148" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer">The NEC’s explanation</a> was simple. 
Voter turnout exceeded expectations, leaving some polling stations short of ballots. 
Under NEC guidelines, local election offices had prepared election-day ballots for roughly half of all registered voters. 
In several precincts, however, far more voters than anticipated turned out on election day, resulting in ballot shortages. 

Yet, its significance extended beyond administrative incompetence. 
The polling stations that ran out of ballots were, coincidentally, 
concentrated in areas where the People Power Party (PPP) was strong. 
Based on <a href="https://www.hankyung.com/article/2026060456627" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer">results from the previous local elections</a>, 
all 14 of those precincts had given more than 50 percent of the vote to Oh Se-hoon, 
the PPP candidate for Seoul mayor. At Jamsil 7-dong Precinct No. 2 in Songpa District, 
Oh’s vote share reached 81.08 percent. Because the shortages were concentrated in opposition strongholds, 
some began to ask whether this was not a mere administrative failure, but deliberate election fraud. 

But such a correlation does not necessarily imply intent. 
The key lies in the gap between early voting and election-day turnout. 
Early voting generally draws relatively more progressive voters. 
By contrast, in conservative areas—strongholds of the PPP, 
now the main opposition—early voting tends to be lower and election-day turnout higher. 
If election-day ballots were prepared uniformly at roughly 50 percent of registered voters in every precinct, 
then a rise in overall turnout could lead to shortages first in areas where the PPP was strongest. 

Still, the NEC should be held accountable for poor election management. 
Voters who waited for hours at polling stations, only to leave in the end, 
could have cast their ballots had the proper supplies been in place. 

Perceptions that the integrity of the election had been compromised spread rapidly, 
particularly among younger voters in their twenties and thirties. 
At more than <a href="https://hanpyo.kr/en" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer">200 universities</a> 
across the country, statements condemning the NEC appeared in succession. 
Students characterized the episode not as a simple administrative error, 
but as a setback for democracy—one that undermined electoral fairness and infringed upon citizens’ right to vote.

The backlash on the streets was even more direct. 
Protesters blockaded Jamsil 7-dong Precinct No. 2, one of the polling stations affected by the ballot shortage. 
Gathering outside the station’s main entrance, they chanted slogans such as 
“Stop the count,” “Nullify the election,” and “Dissolve the Election Commission,” 
while attempting to prevent the ballot boxes from being removed for counting. 

The protests did not end with the police dispersal. Some demonstrators relocated to the Olympic Park Handball Gymnasium, 
the vote-counting center, where they resumed their demonstrations. 
What began as a gathering of roughly one hundred people swelled to 
<a href="https://www.fnnews.com/news/202606071815042648" target="_blank" rel="noreferrer">tens of thousands</a> over the course of the weekend, calling for re-election.

#### Article 195 of the Public Official Election Act

A re-election shall be held when any of following circumstances occurs: 

3. A court judgement or decision has declared the entire election invalid. 

#### Article 224 of the Public Official Election Act

An election commission receiving an election petition, or the Supreme Court or a High Court hearing an election dispute, 
may declare all or part of an election invalid, or invalidate the election of a candidate, 
only when it determines that a violation of election laws or regulations affected the outcome of the election. 
Mere violations of election-related provisions, without an effect on the election result, shall not be sufficient grounds for invalidation.
</TextSection>

Legally, however, the prospect of a rerun appears remote. 
For a new election to be ordered, a court would first have to invalidate the election 
through either an election contest or an election-nullification lawsuit. 
The key legal question is not simply whether procedural irregularities occurred, 
but whether those irregularities affected the outcome. 
In other words, challengers would need to demonstrate that, 
had the voters who were unable to cast ballots because of the shortage actually voted, 
the result of the election could have been different. 

Yet in the major races that encompassed the affected polling stations—
notably the Seoul mayoral election and the Songpa District mayoral election—
the winners were both candidates from the PPP. 
Moreover, the margin between the first- and second-place candidates exceeded the estimated number of voters 
who may have been unable to cast ballots because of the shortage. 
As a result, it is unlikely that a court would find sufficient grounds to invalidate those election results. 

Elections are the foundation of democracy. Yet when serious nonfeasance or misfeasance undermines that foundation, 
the legal system appears to offer few avenues for reaching a remedy commensurate with the gravity of the failure—
one that citizens themselves would regard as satisfactory. 

The NEC was designed as a constitutionally independent body to safeguard political neutrality. 
Yet that very independence has left relatively few institutional mechanisms through which it can be held accountable. 
Independent intended to protect electoral integrity can, when it is perceived to come at the expense of accountability, 
become a source of public distrust. 

This vacuum creates space for other narratives to take hold. 
It invites claims that the government and the ruling party have turned a blind eye to serious failures in election administration, 
and, more extreme, conspiracy theories that those in power are covering up a deliberately manipulated election. 
Indeed, longstanding proponents of election fraud claims—including Hwang Kyo-ahn, Jeon Han-gil, and Morse Tan—
appeared at the protests and portrayed the ballot shortage as evidence of electoral fraud. 

On December 3, 2025, South Koreans had defended their democracy once, when peaceful civic resistance stopped an attempted self-coup. 
Today, that democracy faces another test. 
Democracies do not always collapse under the weight of grand authoritarian threats; 
sometimes they erode through procedures that appear mundane. 
The question now is how we can restore trust in elections as the rules of democratic competition.
